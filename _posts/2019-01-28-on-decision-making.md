I care a lot about my work, it's hard to make a decision with which I don't agree. I feel like it's worth speaking up— but this is challenging when the decision comes from an influential person or leadership / executives.

Of course, many executives say that they want everyone who disagrees to ask questions, but is that something I really want to play on in my career? There is usually a correct way and a wrong way to disagree when it comes to navigating these situations.

When I first hear about a decision I think it's a bad call — a new feature I think is a waste of time, it's a very fragile moment. I often become frustrated or angry, which lead me to give an unproductive and ineffective emotional response.

やれやれめんどくさい someone in the chain thinks it's a good idea; that's why it's done. So, it's worth my time to try to understand the "why" behind this idea — it could change my mind, and even if it doesn't, it gives me the context that I need to make an argument that can convince the decision maker that I'm right.

If there's a decision I'm not in agreement with, I ask about the goal instead of jumping to "no". What is this person is trying to do? If I am not yet in agreement with the explanation, I can try to investigate the problem and provide an alternative plan. I was always taken more seriously when I offered another solution rather than simply saying that the current plan was a bad idea.

Sometimes I can't change the mind of anyone. I shift my perspective and implement the change as described to me. However, there is still an advantage to understand the reasoning behind a change with which I do not agree.

I never say to my team that "because ____ made me do it" is the reason things change. That makes it seem like I have no power and that I'm doing because I was told. I avoid doing things that will not inspire trust in my team or make it less likely to accept the decision.

Instead I try to explain that I am making a change for the reason of XYZ. When asked, I tell our leaders that I shared concerns about the plan. With my team, I always reiterate the reasoning behind the current plan. In the end, it is my job to commit to the strategy of the organization and to help my team succeed in this strategy.

There is never a right answer at work. Each approach has different approaches and tradeoffs. It is not my job to agree 100 percent of the time with the leadership, but it is my job to make the company the most successful.
