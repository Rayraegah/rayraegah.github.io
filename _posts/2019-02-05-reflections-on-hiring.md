A few days ago, an article on hacker news summarized: [When you hire senior engineers, you are not buying, you are selling.](https://hiringengineersbook.com/post/trouble-hiring/)

It made a great deal of sense to me . The important thing for me at work is to know the basics and the end goals of what I do . I never considered syntax or frameworks important . I turn off my internet from time to time to solve problems and give me perspective, "Do I understand the purpose?" and "How can I solve this problem with what I already know?".

I have always sought a sense of belonging. It is  in my DNA and I do it to survive. Beliefs and values have led me all over the world, with several pit stops along the way. Every change I made in my career happened because I was attracted to something bigger than myself. In a way, company  founders and leaders knew how to sell me on their ideas; but how did they manage that?

In 1914 Shackleton prepared to conduct the first Antarctic expedition. No one was lost when the expedition was stuck in the ice and couldn't be rescued for 22 months. The reason for their improbable survival was that Shackleton hired survivors who were in alignment with the mission and purpose.

Where did he find such a crew? He placed an ad on the London Times and it is believed to have read:

<blockquote>
	<p>MEN WANTED FOR HAZARDOUS JOURNEY. SMALL WAGES, BITTER COLD, LONG MONTHS OF COMPLETE DARKNESS, CONSTANT DANGER, SAFE RETURN DOUBTFUL. HONOUR AND RECOGNITION IN CASE OF SUCCESS.</p>
	<footer>SIR ERNEST SHACKLETON</footer>
</blockquote>

The people who applied for this position were entirely enthusiastic about the mission. Can you imagine writing an ad like that to attract the right people to your cause?

It seems that I am always attracted to those who have the same values and beliefs as I do. I am attracted to a company whose values and beliefs align with my own. Since I believe the same things, confidence emerges.

Trust is a feeling, not a rational experience. Trust is not a checklist. The accomplishment of all my tasks does not necessarily build  trust. Even if things go wrong, I trust some people and companies. I elicit trust by communicating and demonstrating that I share the same values and beliefs as the other person.

Great companies do not employ and motivate skilled people, rather they employ and inspire motivated people. People are motivated or are not. If I don't give motivated people something to believe in, something greater than their job, they may instead be motivated to find a different job.

I'm not hiring for skills, I'm hiring for attitudes that match our culture. New skills can always be learned. Success happens when I hire people who believe what we believe. The aim is to hire those who have the  passion and attitude that is right for our culture, purposes, and cause.

I figure out why our good employees fit well, then develop systems to find more. Only when people can trust the organization will they take personal risks to promote it in its entirety. It is ultimately good for their own health and survival.

A strong sense of  work culture creates a sense of belonging and acts like a network. I go to work knowing that my bosses, colleagues, and the organization as a whole will look after me.
