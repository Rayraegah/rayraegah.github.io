In my first few years as an engineer, my peers didn't give me the support, the challenging work or the space to grow to a point where I could comfortably call myself a _software engineer_. So to grow, I had to look beyond the workplace. As a side effect, I branched into different disciplines (because curiosity) such as interaction design, user experience design, and anthropology all the while using code to build tools to solve problems. The result was that I became a generalist rather than a specialist.

In my later years, I had an excellent mentor who constantly cleared roadblocks and threw me in front of the opportunities to grow. I solved problems that I was not qualified to solve, made mistakes, learned on the job, and learned how to approach a problem iteratively to be successful.

So, here are some thoughts about how I think I'm a good mentor and advisor. For me, the most important thing is to remember _who to be_ and not _how to act_. I've yet to remember or learn how to act in a situation. I can look up all the lists I want on tips on what to do, but if I can just _be_ the right person, the choices I'm making and the things I'm talking about come naturally. This was the single reason behind my success in different roles.

However, when it comes down to it, people remember me in a mixture of things I say, facial expressions, body language, and the emotions I project. Often I think a small set of key moments solidifies my mental model into a mentor's brain (and likewise first impressions with all others). Saying the right thing at the right time, or supporting them for the right thing, or challenging them to do better at a receptive moment, can make a mentee all the different.

The key here is being the right person and letting the actions come naturally.

## Being Available

_The Offline Search Engine_

I'm always literally sitting next to anybody I'm mentoring, no exceptions. It's clear to me: if I'm not in earshot, how can I be this person's mentor? I never wear headphones either (at work), if I do then asking me a question is another barrier for them, and I want them to ask me questions.

I constantly remind them that it's my job to be bothered by them and that they can ask me any question every time. This is particularly important in the first few weeks of being onboard. It takes one minute to answer a simple question early that can save them one hour of effort. Doubly so if that effort leads them down the wrong path. As a habit I documented all of the questions and meticulasly wrote down answers in our wiki.

## Being Friendly

_A New Friend_

Being a friendly person, of course, sounds like good general advice, but it is especially important to be extra friendly to a mentee. They've probably been relocated to this new job, so they probably already have a lot of anxiety. Being that local friend literally sitting next to them every day can go a long way in reducing this anxiety.

How to do that? I start by asking them to have lunch all the time. Lunch is so easy, and everybody has to eat anyway. Even if they say no or don't want to have lunch with me, it doesn't really matter. The important part is I invite and remind them that they are welcome to be with me.

At the very least, it's an easy way to anchor the ritual and get everyone in sync plan - wise to carve out a particular time, like a Wednesday team lunch.

I go the extra mile and try to organize other small events, "unofficial offsites" that add a little bit to the team's culture. This could be things like going out for afternoon tea or ice cream on someone's birthday, game night at someone's house, or go to a bar after work. It doesn't have to be a big deal, but it could be a big deal for the new hire that has moved to a new city or country. It can go a long way to make the mentee feel like they're part of the team.

## Make Them Feel Safe

_An Assuring Parent_

Being a good engineer requires judging risks and rewards. Even if my mentee has more experience than I do, they may not know how to assess risks in the context of the organization and team. They may not know how many safeguards we have in place to avoid putting bad code into production. They may not know how fragile a particular subsystem is.

But I don't want my mentee to live in fear, I want them to feel safe. When they are safe, they can explore new solutions. They may be more productive as they push code more frequently. Generally speaking, if they are not afraid to change things, a larger portion of their brain can be dedicated to solving problems and pure creation.

First, I'm explicit on the technical safeguards and say things like:

> The CI/CD tool won’t put your code in production if the integration tests don’t pass, and this thing has plenty of coverage. It basically won’t let you break production.

Then I’ll also assure them from a non-technical perspective and say things like:

> If the coverage wasn't enough, I’ve worked on it and I understand it. If it breaks, we’ll look at it, and I’m sure we can figure it out together.

The nature of the risk at organizations vary. I adjusted as needed.

## Encouraging / Challenging Them Technically

_A Great Storyteller_

Being a really good storyteller requires that I understand the mentee's strength and weaknesses and give them tasks that are challenging enough. There's also some degree of _fun_. If it's too easy it isn't much fun, and if something is brutally hard isn't fun either.

Sometimes things are difficult because they're just unfamiliar. _Simple_ things sometimes seem easy just because they are _at hand_. I try to balance mentees to tasks that are easy for them (although not necessarily easy for me), help them gain confidence early, and then slowly add to more challenging and unfamiliar tasks.

Sometimes, however, they surprise me with their abilities. Sometimes something that I think is hard isn't really hard. Tasks sometimes get tough simply because I prime the mentee's thoughts unnecessarily.

Rather than saying:

> This is going to be a hard issue, it has 5 story points and involves an old part of the codebase.

I say:

> I'm not sure how hard this ticket is, someone probably just gave it 5 story points because they didn't know the code part. Why don't you look at it, maybe spend an hour thinking about how to solve it, and check in with me, and we're going to get together to talk about it.

The other thing I do is make tasks much easier than they seem to be using _code hints_. It can easily change their minds from an intimidated "I don't even know where to start with this" to "oh, there's the code, it looks straightforward."

By giving a code hint, I give a familiar illusion and allow the mentee to focus on the problem itself. Giving mentees _stretch goals_ or _extra credit_ is another way to give them room to show off, gain confidence, and generally give them the opportunity to impress.

I frequently praise them for going the extra mile when a ticket is referenced or closed. I share in their success and mourn with them in their defeats.

## Be a Context-Sharing-Machine

_A Manager?_

I have one-on-one meetings with others including someone I'm mentoring, even though I'm not a manager. It's a dedicated time for them to bring up things they're worried about or proud or confused about.

But I'm actually using this time to reinforce all the things I do during the off-time, but in a private and powerful setting. Words I say can mean a lot in a one-on-one meeting regardless of how many years of experience they have under their belts.

-   Remind them that they are doing a good job and I notice their job.
-   Challenge them to do better
-   Share business context that would be hard to see
-   Share engineering context
